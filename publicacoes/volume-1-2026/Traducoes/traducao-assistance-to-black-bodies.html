Assistance to Black Bodies in Health Establishments:
Deconstructing Prejudices and Promoting an Ethics of Care
Diego Vinicius Brito dos Santos Original translation by the author DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18841714
Baixar PDF DOI Submissões

Abstract

Introduction: This article explores the differential treatment of black bodies in health systems, highlighting the influence of structural racism and gender bias. The differentiated service faced by black individuals is the result of stereotypes rooted in skin color and ethnic origins, worsening when it comes to transgender people and transvestites.

Objective: The study aims to analyze the approach to black bodies in health systems, highlighting the role of unconscious prejudices in the perpetuation of these disparities and proposing critical education as a fundamental step towards awareness.

Method: Narrative review of the literature, analyzing books and articles in electronic journals to identify discriminatory patterns and prejudices.

Results: Discrimination occurs not only consciously but also as a result of social normalization of racism and gender prejudice. Human and critical training is vital to sensitize health professionals.

Conclusion: Urgency of promoting an ethics of care that values diversity. Transformation requires profound cultural change, involving education and critical reflection.

Keywords: Racism; Health Care; Diversity; Critical Training.

1. INTRODUCTION

The scenario of health facilities is the scene of a worrying reality that crosses historical and cultural boundaries: the different treatment given to black bodies (Almeida, 2019 Racismo Estrutural

In the midst of the incessant search for equity in the provision of medical care, a complex web of inequalities and prejudices emerges that profoundly affect the experience and health outcomes of these individuals. This article aims to shed light on the issue of care for black bodies in health systems, exploring the origins of this differentiated treatment, its implications for the health of transgender and transvestite people, the presence of unconscious prejudices and the pressing need for a cultural change that promotes an ethics of care.

Furthermore, gender discrimination intertwines with racism, further compounding the experience of those belonging to both marginalized groups.

The intersectionality theory, coined by Kimberlé Crenshaw (2017) (intersectionality), represents a fundamental approach to understanding the complexity of human experiences and the impacts of social structures. Intersectionality recognizes that an individual’s identities and experiences are shaped not just by one dimension of their identity, such as gender, race, class or sexuality, but by multiple interconnected dimensions.

2. METHODOLOGY

This study adopts a qualitative narrative review approach, examining the cutting edge of the topic. A bibliographical search was conducted in June 2023 across databases such as SCIELO, DOAJ, REDIB, LATINDEX, CAPES Portal. Time limit of 10 years, selecting 19 studies aligned with four analytical axes: 1) Differentiated health care for black bodies; 2) Impacts on black trans and transvestite bodies; 3) Unconscious prejudices; 4) Ethics of care.

3. RESULTS

After filtering, 19 studies were selected: 5 on axis 1, 7 on axis 2, 4 on axis 3 and 3 on axis 4. The findings confirm that structural racism determines negative health outcomes for black populations, affecting access, quality of care, and emotional safety.

4. DISCUSSION

4.1 Differentiated health care for black people

The differentiated care experienced by black bodies is a symptom of systemic problem: structural racism. According to Chehuen Neto et al. (2015), based on the Annual Report on Racial Inequalities in Brazil (2009-2010), there is a clear reduction in quality of life and increase in maternal/infant mortality among the black population. The zone of being and non-being, proposed by Frantz Fanon and revisited by Thula Pires (2018), helps to understand how the health system legitimizes the humanity of white, cisheteronormative bodies while denying black bodies full rights. Health as a fundamental human right is undermined when the "zone of being" dictates who deserves empathy and quality treatment.

4.2 Impacts on trans and transvestite black bodies

According to Rigolon, Oliveira & Salim (2020), the absence of theoretical basis and dialogue during the training of health professionals results in the non-approach of topics related to trans bodies. Intersection of racism and transphobia creates barriers such as refusal of social name, pathologization, and lack of access to primary care. Oliveira (2020) highlights that sexual reassignment processes are often considered low priority, and disrespect for social name causes severe psychological distress.

Thus, black trans women experience simultaneous oppression: they are excluded both by racist structures and by transphobic protocols within health establishments.

4.3 Unconscious prejudices and the “normalization” of racism

Unconscious racism (or "silent racism") is explored by Fernandes (2016) and Almeida (2019). Even well-intentioned professionals can reproduce stereotypes. Racial "jokes", recreational racism, and the naturalization of discriminatory practices are rooted in the collective unconscious inherited from the slavery past. Recognizing these biases is the first step. Critical training and continuing education are essential to deconstruct such mechanisms.

4.4 For an ethics of care in the care of black bodies

The National Policy for Comprehensive Health of the Black Population (NPCHBP) is a milestone. However, Werneck (2016) identified a scarcity of studies on black women's health in Brazilian databases – only six full texts in SciELO. This epistemic gap is also a consequence of unconscious racism. An ethics of care demands humanized, intersectional training, inclusive environments, and the recognition of social determinants of health. The biomedical model based on white, cisheteronormative bodies (Alonso, 2022) must be replaced by a practice that embraces diversity.

5. CONCLUSION

The care for black bodies in health establishments echoes the persistence of a system deeply rooted in racial and gender prejudices. Awareness, education, and promotion of an ethics of care emerge as essential pillars. It is necessary to reform professional curricula, implement anti-racist policies, and foster self-reflection. Only through systemic change can we achieve equitable and respectful health care for all, regardless of skin color, gender identity, or ethnic origin.
